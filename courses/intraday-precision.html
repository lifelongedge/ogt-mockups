Ready Fire Aim — The Intraday Framework

The Ready Fire Aim (RFA) strategy is Dr. Ken Long's intraday system for buying large caps and broad ETFs at key turning points during the day session (9:30 AM – 4:00 PM ET).

The name tells you the protocol:

  • Ready — you've been stalking your target. You have a reason to buy based on tactical momentum. The stock is on your watchlist. The daily chart confirms the setup. SPY is supporting your direction.
  • Fire — you pull the trigger on positive price action and immediately set a capital preservation stop. The entry is mechanical, not emotional. You're in.
  • Aim — you manage the trade carefully. You're prepared to exit on weakness and add a second position on strength. This is where experience and trade efficiency matter most.

RFA is a long-only strategy. Shorts are executed by going long inverse ETFs (like SH, EEV). The key principle: we want to see evidence of price moving in our direction before committing. "Be the second mouse" — the first mouse gets caught in the trap. The second mouse gets the cheese.

The minimum Reward:Risk ratio is 2:1. If you can't frame the trade at 2:1 or better, you don't take it. Period. This single rule eliminates most bad trades before they happen.

Intraday trades typically last 30–60 minutes. The goal is the best return in the shortest time period. Overnight risk is substantial, so we close before the session ends.

The Five Intraday Setups

Dr. Long identifies five recurring patterns on the 5-minute chart. Each takes less than 10 seconds to spot. The preparation — knowing your R, setting your stop, sizing your position — is where the real work happens.

  • 1. The Morning Hook — Stock opens, sells off in the first 30–60 minutes (the "big sell"), then forms a doji or hammer at support. The hook upward is your signal. Enter on the green candle above the doji high.
  • 2. The Intraday Selloff — Same pattern as the Morning Hook, but later in the day. Big sell → doji/hammer → green confirmation candle.
  • 3. The Double Bottom ("W") — Two tests of the same support level. The second test holds (preferably at the same level or slightly higher). Enter on the green candle after the second low confirms.
  • 4. The Shelf (Congestion Zone) — Price is in an uptrend and pauses. Several dojis or narrow-range candles form a flat shelf. The breakout: a green candle closing above the shelf high.
  • 5. The OR5 Breakout — The first 5-minute candle establishes the Opening Range. Price breaking above the OR5 high is the signal. Simple, fast, and effective.

One setup, mastered and repeated consistently, will outperform five setups executed sloppily. Pick one. Run your 20-Trade Challenge. Then add the next.

GYR and the Lunch Rule — Context Drives Everything

Before you look at any individual stock, check one thing: where is SPY relative to yesterday?

Dr. Long uses a color system called GYR (Green-Yellow-Red) to frame the entire day:

Green
SPY above yesterday's high. Expansion mode. Favor trend-continuation setups: shelves, OR5 breakouts, adding to winners. Size up.
Yellow
SPY between yesterday's high and low. Uncertain. Look for 2:1 R:R trades with standard sizing. Adjust as price moves toward Green or Red.
Red
SPY below yesterday's low. Contraction. Favor inverse ETF longs (shorts) or cash. Tighten stops aggressively on any longs.

GYR takes 5 seconds to check. It prevents the single most common intraday mistake: buying long into a Red day because your individual stock "looks good."

The Lunch Rule: Avoid opening new positions between 11:30 AM and 1:30 PM ET. Volume drops, spreads widen, and false signals multiply. If you have an open position going into lunch, tighten your stop or take profit. When you come back at 1:30, you're in a fresh Zero State for the afternoon session.

Dr. Long discovered through his own journal that lunch-hour trades had consistently negative expectancy — approximately $12,000 in avoidable losses per quarter. The journal revealed the pattern. The rule eliminated it.

Scenario

How does the GYR context affect your decision?
SPY is in the Yellow zone, which means caution. A 2:1 R:R is acceptable for Yellow — you don't need 3:1. But size conservatively (standard, not aggressive). The fact that SPY is drifting toward Green is encouraging — if SPY breaks above $576.00 while you're in the trade, consider this a tailwind. If SPY reverses toward Red, tighten your stop immediately regardless of what XLF is doing. Context trumps the individual chart.
