The Hougaard Press — Sizing With Confidence

Most traders size their positions using a simple formula: risk X% of account per trade. That works as a starting point. But the professionals in our coaching program use a more nuanced approach called the Hougaard Press.

The idea comes from a simple observation: your system performs differently in different market conditions. In a trending Summer market, your win rate and average R-multiple are higher. In a choppy Fall market, they're lower. So why would you size the same in both conditions?

The Hougaard Press adjusts your position size based on your system's recent performance. When your equity curve is rising and your R-multiples are consistently positive, you press — you gradually increase size. When your curve flattens or dips, you pull back.

This is psychologically difficult. When you're winning, the instinct is to keep the same size (or double it recklessly). When you're losing, the instinct is to stop entirely. The Hougaard Press does the opposite of what feels natural: it scales methodically based on data, not emotion.

The implementation uses the slope of the RL10 of your cumulative R-multiple as the sizing signal. When the slope is positive and steepening, you're in a favorable regime — press harder. When the slope flattens or turns negative, reduce size. The math removes the emotion.

Dr. Long teaches this progression: start at a base size that feels boring. Only press when the data says your system is in a favorable period. Pull back before the drawdown forces you to.

Question 2 of 3
The RL10 of your cumulative R-multiple is rising steeply. What does this indicate?
A steeply rising RL10 slope means your recent trades are generating consistently positive R-multiples. The system is performing well in current conditions — the data supports a measured increase in size.
Question 3 of 3
Why is the Hougaard Press psychologically difficult?
When you're winning, increasing size feels greedy. When you're losing, reducing feels like giving up. The Hougaard Press requires trusting the data over your instincts — and that takes discipline.

Tracking R — Every Entry Is a Separate Hypothesis

Here's a mistake that costs traders thousands: they enter a position in two pieces and track it as one trade.

Say you buy 500 shares of MSFT at $380 (Trade 1A). Price moves in your favor. You add 250 shares at $383 (Trade 1B). Both are part of the same thesis, but they have different entries, different stops, and different R profiles. If you track them as one blended trade, you lose the ability to measure whether scaling in actually improves your system.

Each entry is a separate hypothesis. Trade 1A says: "MSFT at $380 with a stop at $378 is a favorable R:R." Trade 1B says: "Adding at $383 after confirmation improves my overall result." These are different claims. They deserve different measurement.

Dr. Long insists on labeling every entry separately: 1A, 1B, 1C. Each gets its own entry price, stop, target, and R-multiple calculation. Over 50 or 100 trades, you'll discover whether your scaling-in entries actually have positive expectancy — or whether you'd be better off taking full size on the initial entry.

This discipline also reveals your Trader Quality Number (TQN). Your system has a theoretical edge (SQN — System Quality Number). Your TQN measures how well you personally execute that system. The gap between SQN and TQN is your execution cost. Tracking each entry separately is how you identify where that cost lives.

The 4-3-2-1 Progression — From Paper to Full Size

You have a system with positive expectancy. You've paper traded it for 3 months. You're ready to go live. The question every new trader gets wrong: how much do you start with?

The answer is almost always: less than you think.

Dr. Long teaches a sizing progression called 4-3-2-1:

  • 4 — Paper trade until the setup frequency and execution feel natural. You should be bored by it. If you're still excited by each trade, you haven't paper traded enough.
  • 3 — Quarter position (25% of intended size). This is your first live money. At this level, a -1R loss is trivially small. The goal is to prove you can execute in real markets with real money at stake — order entry, stop placement, position monitoring — without emotional interference.
  • 2 — Half position (50%). Move here when quarter-size feels routine. At half-size, the P&L starts to register emotionally. Pay attention to your state. If you notice anxiety, hesitation, or revenge impulses, you moved up too fast. Drop back to quarter.
  • 1 — Full position. You arrive here when half-size is boring. The full position should feel like a natural extension of the same process, not a dramatic escalation.

The progression takes weeks to months. Rushing it is the most expensive mistake in trading. Every trader who blew up did so because they sized up before their psychology was ready.

Boring is the goal. If your position size makes you feel something, it's too big.

Question 1 of 3
You've been paper trading for 6 weeks and you're still excited by each trade. Are you ready for live trading?
Excitement is a signal that the process hasn't become automatic yet. If each trade still feels like an event, your execution will be emotional. Paper trade until it's boring — that's when you're ready.
Question 2 of 3
At quarter position, you notice your heart racing when you place a stop order. What should you do?
Physical arousal (heart racing) means the dollar risk is too large for your current psychology. The fix is always the same: reduce size until execution feels mechanical. There's no shame in trading smaller — there's only the math.
Question 3 of 3
What is the single most important criterion for moving from half to full position?
The criterion is psychological, not statistical. When half-size losses feel like the cost of doing business — boring, unremarkable — you're ready. Time at a level doesn't matter; emotional readiness does.

Apply What You Learned

Scenario
You paper traded for 2 months with strong results (+0.5R expectancy, 48 trades). You went live at quarter position ($62.50 risk per trade on a $50K account). After 15 live trades, your results: +0.3R expectancy, 7 wins / 8 losses. Your P&L is -$94 (trivial). But you notice you're checking your phone constantly and feeling frustrated after losses. Should you move to half position?
No. The P&L results are fine — slightly positive expectancy with small sample noise. But the behavioral signals say you're not ready: checking phone constantly and frustration after losses are emotional leaks. Stay at quarter position for another 20 trades. Focus on executing the process mechanically. When losses stop generating frustration — when they feel like the cost of doing business — that's when you move to half.
