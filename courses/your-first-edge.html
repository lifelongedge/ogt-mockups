Reading the Market's Season

The market has seasons — just like the year. The MACD Four Seasons framework classifies market conditions into four phases:

Winter
Below Zero, Falling — downtrend, fear dominates. We look for short opportunities.
Spring
Below Zero, Rising — the pain has stopped, recovery begins. We stalk early long entries using the Supported Spring Crossing (SSC).
Summer
Above Zero, Rising — strong uptrend. We hold positions and add on dips.
Fall
Above Zero, Falling — momentum decaying. Harvest time — tighten stops.

The key insight: you don't need to predict what's coming. Recognize which season you're in and align your strategy. A trader who buys in Fall is planting seeds in October.

The MACD uses a 10-period vs 30-period moving average comparison. When the signal line is above zero and rising, that's Summer. When it crosses below zero, Winter arrives. The transitions between seasons — especially Spring after a Harsh Winter — create the highest-probability trading opportunities.

Framing Your Trade

Every trade has three phases. Skipping one is how accounts blow up.

  • Setup — the precondition. The market shows a recognizable pattern: volatility compression, pullback to support, a season transition. This is observation, not action.
  • Signal — the trigger. Something mathematical fires. The MACD hooks upward. Price closes above a prior bar's high. There's no interpretation — it either fired or it didn't.
  • Entry — the execution. You commit capital at a pre-defined price with a pre-defined stop and target. The entry cannot exist without the other two.

Think of a sniper: the setup is choosing your position. The signal is the target entering the crosshairs. The entry is pulling the trigger. A sniper who chases his target across an open field is a dead sniper.

Once you're in, the Rule of Four manages the trade:

  • Give it one — move to no-lose at +1R
  • Aim for three — your rational target
  • Settle for two — lock in +1.5R if momentum stalls
  • Keep one — never give back more than 1R from peak

Question 2 of 3
The MACD hooks upward and price closes above yesterday's high. What phase is this?
A mathematical event fired (MACD hook + price close above prior high). This is a binary, objective trigger — it either happened or it didn't. That's a Signal.
Question 3 of 3
Your trade has reached +2R. According to the Rule of Four, what should you do?
"Settle for two" — at +2R, lock in +1.5R with a trailing stop. You're protecting profit while allowing the trade to continue toward the 3R target.

Sizing Your Position

Ask a new trader about their last trade and they'll tell you what they bought. Ask a professional and the first thing they say is: "How much did I risk?"

1R = the amount you're willing to lose on a single trade. It's a fixed dollar amount calculated from your system's edge and your own psychology.

If your account is $50,000 and you risk 0.5% per trade, your 1R = $250. If your initial stop is $0.10 from entry, you buy 2,500 shares. Not 100. Not 10,000. The stop drives the math.

Position size is also a psychological signal. If 2,500 shares makes your heart race, the position is too big — regardless of what the math says. The goal is to find the size where a -1R loss is genuinely acceptable and you can execute your plan without emotional interference.

The progression: paper trade first, then 25% size, then 50%, then full. Boring is the goal. If your position size makes you feel something, it's too big.

Question 1 of 3
Your account is $100,000. You risk 0.5% per trade. What is your 1R?
$100,000 × 0.5% = $500. This is your maximum acceptable loss per trade. Every position size calculation starts from this number.
Question 2 of 3
Your 1R is $500. The initial stop on your trade is $2.00 below entry. How many shares do you buy?
$500 ÷ $2.00 per share = 250 shares. The stop distance drives the position size, not the other way around. Never choose shares first and then figure out where to put the stop.
Question 3 of 3
You're at 250 shares and your heart is racing. What should you do?
Emotional arousal during a trade impairs decision-making. If the size feels uncomfortable, the math doesn't matter — cut it back until a -1R loss is genuinely acceptable. Boring is the goal.

Apply What You Learned

Scenario
You've been paper trading for 2 months and your system shows +0.4R expectancy over 40 trades. You're ready to go live with a $25,000 account. Walk through the sizing decision.
At 0.5% risk, your 1R = $125. Start at 25% of intended size (quarter position). If your system calls for 500 shares of a $25 stock with a $0.25 stop, trade 125 shares instead. When quarter-size feels routine, move to 250 shares. When that's boring, go to full 500. The progression takes weeks, not days. Your paper-trading edge (+0.4R over 40 trades) gives you confidence that the system works — now the only variable is your execution psychology.
