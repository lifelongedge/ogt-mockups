MMRB

The Minimum Manageable Risk Box (MMRB) is the smallest stop distance — about one-tenth of the 30-day average range — that you can defend without being shaken out by noise, so a -1R loss is a tiny fraction of your account.

Ken’s framing

Ken’s working definition (Mvp foundations Q&A, ~00:44:11): “the MMRB that we use now is by definition one-tenth of the range stat.” The range stat is one standard deviation of the average range of the last 30 days. He immediately ties it to position sizing (Mvp ~00:44:34): “if I take a minus 1R loss on an MMRB, I’m going to set that equal to a 0.2% loss mathematically.” And he names its purpose plainly (Mvp ~00:42:48): “we’re taking all our standard patterns and trying to figure out how to squeeze the minimum manageable risk box down to the smallest level so that the amount of risk that I take or uncertainty is small” — that is the essence of sniper trading. Foundation Book Session 6 calls the per-share version “Rstat / MyRisk” (p. 181); Session 10 notes the morning hook, Rstat, and MMRB collapse into one slide.

Why it matters

MMRB is the unit Ken uses to convert every chart pattern into a measurable risk-to-reward equation: stop distance, position size, the 2R battle drill, the “no-lose plus dinner for two” first scale, and the “hang an MMRB off the high” trailing exit are all defined in MMRBs. Without MMRB the student cannot price a setup, cannot size a sniper trade, and cannot decide whether to give back risk on a winner. It is the atomic risk unit underneath sniper, kata reentry, R10 stops, and frog box scaling.

Try it yourself

Pick three symbols from your current watchlist (a high-priced, a mid-priced, and a sub-$50 name). For each, look up the 30-day average range (range stat) on the daily chart and compute MMRB = range_stat / 10. Then open the 5-minute or 10-minute intraday chart and physically draw a horizontal band of that width above and below the current price — that is your MMRB. Finally, size a hypothetical trade so a 1R loss equals 0.2% of your account, and write down (a) what you would enter at, (b) where the MMRB stop sits, and (c) where 2R, 5R, and 9R targets land. Compare across the three symbols: which one gives you the cleanest “tiny risk, lots of headroom” picture? That is the sniper candidate.
