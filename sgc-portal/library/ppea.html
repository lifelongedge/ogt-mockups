PPEA

PPEA — Plan, Prepare, Execute, Assess — is Ken Long’s top-level four-phase process that governs every trading action, from system design to the next live trade.

Ken’s framing

Ken introduces PPEA in the Foundation Trading Book (Session 3, pgs. 107–112) as “a 4-step process to improve your adaptation systematically and continuously to the market and trade effectively.” He defines each phase precisely: Plan is the top-down procedure to identify tradeable targets in a critical state (the least important phase because it is easiest to standardize); Prepare is the bi-directional due diligence — decision points, key price levels, rehearsals for contingencies in both directions — “ten times harder than error-free execution”; Execute is implementing the rehearsed plan with emphasis on exits; Assess is the routine and rigorous evaluation of each trade, “where you want to be at your best, because this is where you can LEARN.” In the Book Club opener he reframes the four phases as “really two phases — a plan-prepare phase and an execute-assessment phase” and tells students explicitly that PPEA “is the top-level model that governs all our actions, whether it’s developing a new system, doing psychological self-work, preparing for a particular trade, or learning some new skills.”

Why it matters

PPEA is the operating system underneath every other Edge concept — RLCO, the Sniper trade, MMRB, the daily report, AAR — they all plug into one of the four phases. A student who treats PPEA as a slogan stays a discretionary gambler; a student who runs the loop turns each trade into a unit of learning rather than a unit of P&L variance. Ken’s claim that “the more you plan, prepare, execute, and assess, the luckier you’re going to become” is the closest he comes to a single sentence explanation for how disciplined traders compound an edge that random traders cannot.

A 4-step process to improve your adaptation systematically and continuously to the market and trade effectively. Plan: top-down procedure to identify tradeable targets… PREPARE: the due diligence to develop a bi-directional trading strategy — decision points with criteria for action, key price levels, and rehearsals to practice for contingencies in both directions. Good preparation is ten times harder than error-free execution. Execute: implement and measure the rehearsed prepared plan. Emphasis on exits. ASSESS: routine and rigorous evaluation of each trade. This is where you want to be at your best, because this is where you can LEARN.

Foundation Trading Book_Cornell Notes_Session3_v1.pptx — Trade Framing Process — pgs. 113–114 (the in-the-trade tooling that lives inside Prepare)

In order to be fully prepared for the trade we must frame the trade in both directions and ensure that we have estimated our reward to risk. A standardized way to review all of the typical decisions that occur in a trade like exit rules, when to add second positions, when to stop and reverse, and when to leave the target alone. This can help us avoid impulsive actions to trade our feelings when we are surprised by an opportunity. The discipline can be learned quickly but takes hundreds of examples to fully master.

Try it yourself

Pick one symbol from tomorrow’s watchlist and write a single page that splits cleanly into four headed sections: Plan (why this symbol passed your scan and what critical state you think it is in), Prepare (frame the trade in BOTH directions — entry triggers, stop, first profit target, where you stop-and-reverse, and the one or two contingencies you’d be surprised by), Execute (the literal one-page battle drill you will read while the bar is forming — no new thinking allowed), and Assess (the three questions you will ask yourself after the close regardless of P&L: did I follow the drill, what did the market actually do versus what I expected, and what one thing will I change in tomorrow’s Prepare). Tomorrow, trade only that one symbol and only that drill. The next morning, write the Assess section before you look at any new charts — and then assess your assessment: did you grade your process honestly or did you grade your outcome?
