RLCO

Regression Line Cross Over — the moment a fast regression line (RL10) crosses its slower partner (RL30) outside the Bollinger “river,” giving an early read on whether a trend will continue or reverse.

Ken’s framing

Ken describes the RLCO as a statistical lens, not a signal. “The RL10 is the trader’s price… the RL30 is a slower version of that — the direction of the RL30 is the health of the trend” (swing-details-riskz, 00:21:43). The framework lays a 10-period and 30-period regression line over a 30-period Bollinger Band (“the river”); when both lines push outside the river the market is in an “extreme” condition, and the crossover of RL10 back through RL30 marks the earliest probabilistic clue that the herd may reverse. The canonical SGC write-up (RLCO SetUp v0.docx) calls it “a momentum based setup that relies on technical indicators to gauge the momentum of a price trend in the medium and short term,” distinguishing two outcomes: trend continuation when momentum stays strong, trend reversal when momentum decays.

Why it matters

RLCO is the conceptual root of almost everything Ken teaches downstream — Four Seasons / MACD is “RLCO simplified” (strategies-13-22, 00:44:21), the Owl signals are RL30 outside Z1 (masterclass, 00:02:25), and the fractal framework (RL10/30/90/270) extends the same logic across timeframes. A student who internalizes RLCO can read a chart the way Ken does: as a story about traders, trend health, fair value, and the river they’re crossing. Skipping it leaves every later concept ungrounded.

SGC book anchors

RLCO SetUp v0.docx — The RLCO Concept + Statistical Background + Zebras and Rivers metaphor

The typical time frame used in the charts is 3 minute, although it can be used in any time frame. There are four indicators involved: Regression line with look back 10 (RL(10)), Regression line with period 30 (RL(30)), Bollinger bands period 30 at +/- 1 SD, and VWAP. The extreme readings by the RLCO are given when both of the regression line curves cross outside the region delimited by the upper and lower BB. ‘A RLCO outside the river means both RLs are outside. The RL(10) changes direction first and moves back to the river, while the RL(30) is still moving away — that’s the initial signal that the whole herd may change.’

rlco_notes.docx — Top 10 relevant points about the RLCO system

Disciplined: an overlay that can be applied to different systems and time frames. Multiple timeframes: the RLCO lens applies from minute charts to weekly and monthly. Consistency: regardless of market, timeframe, market and direction. Finds critical moments in all market conditions. Applies in all 3 directions: helps us when price is moving up, down and sideways. Forward looking: regression lines help forecasters assess future possibilities with confidence intervals.

Foundation Trading Book_Cornell Notes_Session6_v1.pptx — 30-Period Regression Line Slopes (pgs. 180-181) — sector application

We can use the slope of the 30 period regression line to find sectors that are interesting or sectors to stay away from. In this example, XLE and XLF had peaked at +1.99 sigma and was starting to fall, showing a declining health in the trend. … TSLA, AA, DVN — JPM we were able to discard because of the weakness we spotted on the RL30 slopes on the financial sector XLF.

Try it yourself

Open any 30-minute chart of a symbol you traded last week. Plot the RL10, the RL30, and a 30-period Bollinger Band at +/- 1 SD (call the BB region “the river”). Walk the day bar by bar and mark every spot where RL10 crosses RL30 outside the river. For each cross, write one sentence answering: was the RL30 still pointed away from the river mean (continuation likely) or already pointed back toward it (reversal likely)? Then check what price actually did over the next 6 bars. Do this for 3 days; you will start seeing the herd before it turns.
